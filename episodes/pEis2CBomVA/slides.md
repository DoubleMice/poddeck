# The Biggest Mistake: Not Knowing When It Starts

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">Negotiation begins long before you get the offer letter</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500">

### Your Public Perception
Your LinkedIn profile, headshot, and narrative define how people see you. Jacob says he could predict someone's salary by the quality of their headshot — and it was "wildly consistent."

<div class="mt-2 italic text-sm">"If you're positioned as a commodity, you will be treated like a commodity"</div>
</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500">

### Recruiter Conversations Are Permanent
When a recruiter asks how much you expect, that number follows you. Jacob: a recruiter who heard "225" will offer you 225 again five years later, not recognizing your growth.

<div class="mt-2 italic text-sm">"everything you communicate starts to serve as that value chain across the board"</div>
</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500">

### The Less You Share, The Better
When you're on the phone, you can correct the narrative and steer the conversation. But once you've shared a number or a title, you're anchored to it. Control the information flow from day one.
</div>

# Home Field Advantage: Control Time & Place

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">Where and when you negotiate matters more than you think</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500">

### Control the Environment
More deals are closed on the golf course than in boardrooms. Pull the conversation out of their office — coffee, lunch, a walk. Walking side by side is collaborative; sitting across is confrontational.

<div class="mt-2 italic text-sm">"instead of you and I being confrontational like negotiating between each other, we're walking side by side"</div>
</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500">

### Control the Time
CEO wants to meet at 6 AM? Say no. Know when your body language is strongest. Jacob recommends: "I'm strongest between the hours of 10:00 a.m. and 1:00 p.m." — this also communicates scarcity.

<div class="mt-2 italic text-sm">"That also communicates scarcity and that you won't get pushed around"</div>
</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500">

### The OpenAI Walk
Jacob's clients interviewing for C-suite roles at OpenAI would walk downtown San Francisco instead of meeting in the office. The real reason? The office was chaotic — but the side effect was better negotiation dynamics.
</div>

# The Number Question: Who Speaks First?

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">When they ask "how much do you want to make?" — here's what to do</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded border-l-4 border-blue-500 bg-blue-50 bg-opacity-30">

### The Deflection Script

<div class="text-sm mt-2 space-y-2">

**Step 1**: "I don't talk about compensation until we're ready to make an offer. I'd love the opportunity to learn more about the team."

**Step 2** (if they push): "I'm uncomfortable doing that right now because I don't understand the scope of the role. Can you help me understand what you had in mind?"

**Step 3**: Turn the question back — "What did you have in mind?" flips the anchor to them.

</div>
</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded border-l-4 border-green-500 bg-green-50 bg-opacity-30">

### Why This Works

<div class="text-sm mt-2">

**Never be so sure of your worth that you wouldn't accept more.** When you anchor, that becomes the ceiling. They'll naturally split the difference.

The key insight: "How much do you want to make?" is eerily similar to the illegal question "How much do you make?" — they just reword it.

If you must share a number, **anchor egregiously high**. Studies show those who anchored high won 75% more than those who tried to be reasonable.

</div>
</div>

# Don't Anchor Too Early: The Scope Creep Trap

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">Why committing to a number before understanding the role costs you</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500 rounded mt-4">

### The Trap in Action

You anchor to "this is fair for a senior PM role." Then through interviews, the scope expands: lead five people, add a product-led growth motion, an engineering pod, more teams. Classic **scope creep**. When the offer comes in 20% under your original number, they say: "But you said you only wanted 140."

<div class="mt-2 italic text-sm">"they scope creep the role... then they give you the offer and it's actually 20% under the number you originally shared"</div>
</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded">

### The Fix
Treat the process like an enterprise B2B sale. You are a multi-hundred-thousand-dollar consulting solution. You wouldn't pitch the price before discovery — so don't share your number before understanding the role.
</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

### The Reframe
"As we had this conversation, I'm likely a little more horsepower than you anticipated. What's the chance we can come in and restructure the comp for the type of talent we're bringing in here?"
</div>

</div>

# The Spielberg Close: Act Like You Already Have the Job

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">A Hollywood story that illustrates the most powerful negotiation move</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded mt-4">

### The Setup
A director of a billion-dollar music tour was the hottest commodity in Hollywood. Every agency wanted to sign them. Most agencies pitched credentials: "We've represented Julia Roberts, Matt Damon..."

Jacob's insight: **leaning on logic and credibility loses. You need to sell the future and own the emotion.**

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded mt-3">

### The Move
The director asked: "Why should I sign your agency?" Jacob's client responded: **"What are you doing on Friday? I cleared a lunch with your assistant."** He asked "Stephen who?" She said **"Stephen Spielberg."** Deal closed.

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded mt-3 text-sm">

### The Principle
"If you already had the job, who would you introduce him to?" — Don't pitch from the outside. Put yourself in the position that you're already doing the job.
</div>

# The Werewolf Game: Information Asymmetry

Jacob uses the game of Werewolf to explain why information is the key to negotiation power.

<div class="mt-3 text-sm">

**The parallel**: Companies negotiate thousands of times a day. You negotiate 4-5 times in your career. They know all salaries, budgets, and alternatives. You're the villager.

</div>

<div class="mt-3 p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 text-sm">

**The upside**: This should give you confidence to push back. You're already at a massive disadvantage — you have nothing to lose by trying.

# The Severance Story: "Was That a Mistake?"

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">A tiny word difference worth $90,000+ — and why you must read the fine print</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded mt-4">

### The Setup
A Chief Revenue Officer was paid $350K base + $350K bonus = $700K on-target earnings. They negotiated six months of **on-target earnings** as severance. But the paperwork came back saying six months of **base salary** — cutting severance in half.

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500 rounded">

### The Instinct (Wrong)
The CRO wanted to split the difference — propose something around $260K. Would have cost him ~$90K just from lazy negotiating.
</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

### The Fix (Right)
Jacob said: simply ask **"Was that a mistake?"** The CEO responded: "Oh yeah it must have been. Let me just tell the attorneys to fix it. Feel free to redline it."
</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 text-sm">

**Postscript**: 12 months later, the CRO got fired — and recouped $350,000 over 6 months thanks to the corrected severance terms. That one question was worth six figures.
</div>

# The CMO Severance Story: Make the Company Look Good

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">How to negotiate for yourself by negotiating for everyone</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded mt-4">

### The Situation
A CMO joins a $200M private company. CEO prides himself on equitable pay: everyone gets $340K base, 30% bonus, same equity. No room to push on cash.
</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded mt-3">

### The Move
Instead of negotiating personal severance (awkward — it's like negotiating divorce terms before the wedding), they proposed: **"Wouldn't it make us look good to proactively give everyone on the executive leadership team six months severance to show that they're safe and committed?"**

Fed the idea to the CEO so the CEO could look like the hero.
</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded mt-3">

### The Result
The CMO got her severance protection. But more importantly — every executive got it too. She walked in as "the woman that negotiated us all severance protections." She used the company's own equitable policies to expand the benefit for everyone.

# When Things Go South: The Rescinded Offer

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">A cautionary tale about authenticity and the power of vulnerability</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500 rounded mt-4">

### The Disaster
A CMO was offered an $800K contract. Her career coach told her to "negotiate like a white man" — be aggressive, demand your worth. Problem: she was negotiating with a female CEO and three other women on the leadership team. They rescinded the offer because she seemed like a bad cultural fit.
</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded mt-3">

### The Recovery
Jacob's advice: call the CEO, be honest. Share the identity: "it's important that women take care of each other... I got to be honest with you, I hired a coach... I made a mistake and I disagreed with the advice, but I did it anyway because I had to try."

**Shared identity + extreme ownership + transparency** brought the offer back on the table. She's now happily making $800K/year.
</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded mt-3 text-sm">

**The lesson**: There's no one-size-fits-all approach. Context matters — who you're negotiating with, the culture, the dynamics. Authenticity beats persona every time.
</div>

# Hollywood Scale: From $5M to $22M in Two Days

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">How negotiation works at the highest levels — and what it teaches everyone</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="mt-2 text-sm">They moved **$4 million chunks at a time** in a two-day span. Meanwhile, tech professionals agonize over asking for $10K more.</div>
</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded border-l-4 border-green-500 bg-green-50 bg-opacity-30">

### The Takeaway
Scale matters. Jacob puts it in perspective: a million seconds is 11 days. A billion seconds is **31 years**. The discrepancy between small and big negotiations isn't linear — it's exponential.

<div class="mt-2 text-sm italic">"if you want to really increase your comp, you do have to move rooms... start networking and making connections with folks that can do that with you"</div>
</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500">

**The flip side**: If you're making $18/hour and negotiate to $24, that's equally life-changing. Jacob remembers being homeless 12 years ago, fighting to pay for gas. The principles work at every level — the dollar amounts just change.

# The "Fingerprintless" Advisor Model

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">How Jacob works behind the scenes without ever entering the room</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded mt-4">

Jacob calls his approach **"fingerprintless"** — similar to an attorney, not a Hollywood agent. All correspondence goes through him before it reaches the other side.

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-3 gap-3 mt-4 text-sm">

<div class="p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

### 1. Prepare
Client calls Jacob before any conversation. They strategize the game plan together.
</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded">

### 2. Perform & Debrief
Client has the conversation, then reports back. They analyze what happened.
</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded">

### 3. Draft Together
CEO sends an email → Jacob reviews it → they draft the response together, testing the client's instincts first.
</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-3 p-3 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 text-sm">

**Why slow down?** "Haste equals risk." Taking days to respond communicates scarcity, shows thoughtfulness, and gives time to collect information and build a compelling case.
</div>

# For Product People: You Already Know How to Do This

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">Jacob reframes negotiation in terms product leaders already understand</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded">

### Product = Negotiation
You know that an inbound lead is easier to close than outbound. You understand user research, pain points, eliminating friction. A job search is the same product flow:

- **Your buyer** = hiring manager / CPO / CEO
- **User research** = discovery questions in interviews
- **Friction elimination** = making it frictionless to hire you
</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

### Don't Be Jacob
"Don't be Jacob Light. Be you, your authentic you, and apply it to what you already know." The early days of managing your career like a product will feel rough — like early days of writing PRDs. But 10 years in, it becomes second nature.
</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500">

### Build Champions
Like an enterprise sales process, build multiple champions inside the organization. Ask to talk to engineering, marketing, sales leads. "I'm making it frictionless across the process and I'm closing the next part of the conversation regardless of whether you wanted me to introduce me to those other people or not."
</div>

# The Power of "No" and the G-Wagon

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">Having the ability to say no gives you infinite power — and sometimes leads to creative solutions</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded">

### The Power Dynamic
If you have a job and your needs are met, you can say no — and that gives you infinite power. The nuance changes when you need the paycheck. This is why it's easier to find a job when you have one: your needs are met, so you negotiate more confidently.

<div class="mt-2 italic text-sm">"if somebody disrespects you through that process, doesn't that give you an indication of who the company's working with?"</div>
</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

### The G-Wagon Story
A CEO had two identical offers at $2.4M. Both maxed out. Stalemate. Jacob jokingly asked what car she liked. "Mercedes." She mentioned it to the company. They said "Done. What model?" A $350K G-Wagon was added as a company write-off — 6,000lb vehicle tax deduction.

<div class="mt-2 text-sm opacity-70">Jacob's caveat: "I don't suggest people go ask for G Wagons" — but when you hit a wall, think creatively.</div>
</div>

# The Cystic Fibrosis Foundation

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">Jacob's most personal cause — and why networking for good matters</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded mt-4">

Jacob's son has cystic fibrosis — a genetic condition where life expectancy used to be 7-10 years. New medication has changed that expectancy from 7 to 70 years. He serves on the board of the Cody de Roof Foundation in Bozeman, Montana, helping rural communities access healthcare.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded mt-3">

### The Story That Got Him
A mother had three boys with cystic fibrosis. Her 29-year-old needed a lung transplant — she couldn't afford it, and he passed away. Two years later, her 27-year-old needed the same surgery. She found the foundation in time — they funded the Lifeflight to Denver Children's Hospital and saved his life.

The part that broke Jacob: the mother now carries guilt that she could have saved her first son if she'd known the foundation existed.
</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded mt-3 text-sm">

Within 24 hours of his son's diagnosis, Jacob's network delivered: calls from Denver Children's Hospital, Stanford, and the FDA. All those years of "give before you ask" came back when it mattered most.
