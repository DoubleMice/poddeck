# The 2D:4D Digit Ratio

In 1999, Breedlove read a paper showing a **sex difference in finger length ratios** present in 9-year-old children — before puberty.

- **2D:4D ratio** = index finger length ÷ ring finger length
- Tends to be **smaller in men** than women (ring finger relatively longer)
- More pronounced on the **right hand**
- Present before puberty → almost certainly due to **prenatal testosterone**

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded text-sm italic">

"A sex difference in the body that's present before puberty. I know enough about sexual differentiation of the body, it's almost certainly due to prenatal testosterone."

</div>

# The Street Fair Study

Breedlove's team took their research to the streets of the San Francisco Bay Area — quite literally.

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500">

### Method
- Street fairs in the Castro (gay men), Solano/Oakland (lesbians), and mixed events
- Anonymous questionnaires + hand photocopies
- Incentive: **$1 lottery scratcher tickets**
- Breedlove measured all digits himself — twice

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500">

### Key Findings
- **Gay vs straight men**: no significant difference in digit ratios → similar prenatal testosterone
- **Lesbians vs straight women**: lesbians had **more masculine digit ratios** — replicated by multiple labs worldwide
- Implication: lesbians on average exposed to slightly more prenatal testosterone

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 rounded text-sm italic">

"It was the least expensive experiment I'd ever done in my life... 'Please, I'd like 750 $1 lottery scratcher tickets.'"

</div>

# Don't Panic: Group Stats vs. Individual Prediction

<div class="mt-2 text-sm opacity-70">A crucial lesson in reading statistics that applies far beyond this study</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500">

### The Height Analogy
- Sex difference in height: **2 standard deviations** apart
- If you guess sex from height alone, you're right ~80% of the time
- Still wrong **20%** of the time — and that's the *biggest* sex difference

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500">

### The Digit Ratio Reality
- Sex difference in 2D:4D: only **0.5 standard deviations**
- A quarter the size of the height difference
- Other factors also influence digit ratios
- **Zero predictive value** for any individual

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500 rounded text-sm italic">

"Look at the right hand, pay really careful attention... and no matter what you see, guess straight and you will be right 95% of the time."

<div class="text-xs opacity-60 mt-1 not-italic">— Breedlove's joke about the limits of individual prediction</div>
</div>

# Otoacoustic Emissions: The Ear Evidence

<div class="mt-2 text-sm opacity-70">Dennis McFadden at UT Austin — the study that changed Breedlove's mind about prenatal hormones</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500">

### What Are They?
- Your ears make tiny popping sounds you can't perceive
- Detectable with sensitive microphones in soundproof rooms
- Help you focus on the sounds you want to hear
- **Sex difference present at birth**: girls produce more

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500">

### McFadden's Discovery
- Compared straight men, gay men, straight women, lesbians
- **Lesbians had fewer** otoacoustic emissions than straight women
- Shifted toward the male pattern
- Present at birth → can't be social learning
- Best explanation: lesbians exposed to more **prenatal testosterone**

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded text-sm">

This was 1998. Breedlove had spent decades writing NIH grants claiming early hormones "may be important for human behavior" — but privately never believed it. McFadden's paper changed everything.

</div>

# Simon LeVay's Brain Study

Simon LeVay, an already respected neuroscientist, examined the **preoptic area** of the hypothalamus in gay vs. straight men.

- A nucleus there (INAH-3 / SDNPOA) is **larger in men than women** in rats and humans
- In gay men: the nucleus was **smaller** than in straight men
- Not significantly different from the size in women
- Replicated by William Byne's group — despite their skepticism

<div class="mt-3 p-3 bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500 rounded text-sm">

**Chicken-and-egg problem**: Were they gay because of a smaller SDNPOA? Or did the SDNPOA shrink *because* they were gay? The adult brain changes all the time — we can't assume causation from a post-mortem snapshot.

</div>

# The Romeo Rat & 60 Minutes

Breedlove on CBS's 60 Minutes — demonstrating how neonatal castration permanently changes sexual behavior.

<div class="grid grid-cols-3 gap-4 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-2 border-blue-200 text-center">

### Step 1
Drop a **receptive female** in with Romeo (experienced male rat). He mounts, she shows lordosis. Textbook.

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-yellow-50 border-2 border-yellow-200 text-center">

### Step 2
Drop a **control male** in. Romeo mounts him several times. The control male acts like nothing is going on. Romeo gives up.

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-2 border-green-200 text-center">

### Step 3
Drop in a male **castrated at birth** + given female hormones. Romeo mounts — and gets **beautiful lordosis**. Permanent behavioral change.

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded">

<div class="text-sm italic">"Would you say this is a gay rat?" — Leslie Stahl kept asking.</div>
<div class="text-sm mt-2">Breedlove's answer: "I don't think my rats have an orientation." Romeo would mount *any* rat. What humans care about — the **identity of the partner** — is what makes us distinctive.</div>

</div>

# Humans Are Different: Partner Identity Matters

<div class="mt-1 text-sm opacity-70">The key insight that separates human sexuality from animal models</div>

<div class="mt-3 p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded text-sm italic">

"We're overwhelmingly interested in who our partner is. That is an overriding concern that I don't think my rats have."

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 mt-3 text-sm">

<div class="p-3 rounded bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500">

### Animals
- Motor patterns well-studied (lordosis, mounting)
- Rats don't have partner *preference* — Romeo mounts anything
- We know motor circuits well, **much less about motivation**

</div>

<div class="p-3 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500">

### Humans
- Biggest sex differences are in **sexual attitudes**, not cognition
- Gay men are "totally masculine" in these attitudes
- The real question: **who** is the partner, not **what** acts occur

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-2 text-xs opacity-60">

Thought experiment: half the planet finds George Clooney an ideal partner; the other half finds him totally unacceptable — even though the behaviors would be the same.

</div>

# Gay Rams: Real Orientation in Animals

Chuck Roselli at Oregon discovered that **~8% of rams** never mount females and exclusively mount males.

- Shepherds have known this for ages
- In controlled tests: a gay ram placed with a dozen receptive females for 12 hours — **never mounts a single one**
- Full intromission and ejaculation with males
- Not a lack of opportunity — it's an active **aversion** to females

<div class="mt-3 p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded text-sm italic">

"I'm going to call them gay rams. I think they have an orientation."

</div>

<div class="mt-2 text-sm">

The kicker: Roselli found a difference in the **preoptic area** of gay vs. straight rams — the same brain region LeVay found different in gay vs. straight men.

</div>

# The Aversion Hypothesis

<div class="mt-2 text-sm opacity-70">Sexual orientation isn't just attraction — it may involve active aversion circuits</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500">

### The Push-Pull Model
Across neuroscience: flexor/extensor, hunger/satiety, attraction/**aversion**

- Gay rams don't just prefer males — mating with females appears **aversive**
- In heterosexual men: the idea of same-sex mating is often actively aversive, not just neutral
- This mirrors the push-pull architecture seen throughout the brain

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500">

### Sex Difference in Plasticity
- **Women are more plastic** in sexual orientation
- More women report bisexuality; some shift orientation over their lifetime
- Huberman's hypothesis: men may have a stronger aversion circuit; women may lack it or have a weaker version
- Breedlove's response: "Your hypothesis fits the data"

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-3 p-3 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded text-sm">

**Why this matters**: Understanding the aversion pathway is as important as understanding attraction for making sense of human sexuality — and for advancing the conversation past politics.

</div>

# Fraternal Birth Order Effect

One of the most replicated findings in human sexuality — first noticed by Ray Blanchard at Toronto.

<div class="mt-3 text-sm">

| Older brothers | Probability of being gay |
|---|---|
| 0 | ~2% |
| 1 | ~2.6% (+33%) |
| 2 | ~3.5% (+33%) |
| ... | linear increase |
| ~12 | ~50% |

</div>

<div class="mt-3 p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded text-sm">

For straight men: **105 older brothers** per 100 older sisters (matches birth ratio). For gay men: **140 older brothers** per 100 older sisters.

</div>

<div class="mt-2 text-sm opacity-70">

Blanchard estimates about **one in seven** gay men are gay because their mother carried brothers before them.

</div>

# It's Not Social — It's the Mother's Body

<div class="mt-2 text-sm opacity-70">Tony Bogaert's elegant natural experiments separate biology from upbringing</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500">

### What Doesn't Matter
- **Step brothers**: no effect on orientation
- **Older sisters**: no effect
- **Younger brothers**: no effect
- Being **raised together** or apart: no difference
- Dad leaving the household: no correlation found

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500">

### What Does Matter
- **Biological older brothers from the same mother** — even if raised apart
- It's the mother's body "remembering" how many sons she carried
- The effect is biological, not socially mediated
- Each delivery of a son exposes the mother's immune system to **male-specific antigens**

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded text-sm">

This finding inverts the folk explanation: people assumed having older *sisters* or a missing father might make a boy gay. The biology says the exact opposite — it's older *brothers* that matter, via an immunological mechanism.

</div>

# Maternal Immunization Hypothesis

The Blanchard-Bogaert mechanism that explains *how* older brothers increase the probability.

1. First son in utero → mother's immune system never encounters Y-chromosome antigens
2. At birth → blood mixing → mother's immune system sees **male-specific antigens** for the first time
3. Antibodies generated → specifically to **neuroligin-4Y** (a synapse formation protein on the Y chromosome)
4. Next son → maternal antibodies cross the placenta and may **perturb brain development**
5. Each subsequent son → more antibodies → incrementally higher odds

<div class="mt-3 p-3 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 rounded text-sm">

Women whose later sons showed the pattern have **higher levels of antibodies** to neuroligin-4Y — a male-specific synapse protein.

</div>

# CAH: Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia

<div class="mt-2 text-sm opacity-70">A natural experiment in prenatal testosterone exposure</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500">

### The Mechanism
- Fetus can't make certain adrenal steroids
- Brain tells adrenal glands to produce more
- Adrenals hypertrophy but can't make the right steroids
- Instead they produce **testosterone and other androgens**
- In XX individuals: intersex phenotype at birth

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500">

### Sexual Orientation in CAH Women
- More likely to be **same-sex attracted** than the general population
- But most are still straight
- The older they get, the **higher the percentage** reporting lesbian orientation
- Consistent with prenatal testosterone increasing the odds of attraction to women

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded text-sm">

**Heterozygous carriers** (one mutant copy) are surprisingly common: **1 in 12** people. They may have subtle phenotypes — slightly more androgens, altered stress response — but it's not well studied.

</div>

# AIS: Androgen Insensitivity Syndrome

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500">

### The Biology
- XY individual with a dysfunctional androgen receptor (gene on X chromosome)
- Testes form, release testosterone — but the body **can't respond**
- Anti-Mullerian hormone suppresses female duct development
- External appearance: **typically female**
- Often undetected until puberty (no period)

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500">

### The Orientation Question
- Vast majority grow up **attracted to men** — very feminine, often interested in having families
- But: are they straight because their brain never responded to testosterone? Or because they were **raised as girls**?
- AIS can't disentangle nature from nurture here — both explanations fit
- A frustrating limitation for researchers

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 rounded text-sm">

With no testosterone response, AIS individuals often have very feminine faces and bodies. At least one woman with AIS is a successful model; another wrote a well-known memoir.

</div>

# Gynandromorphs: When One Side Is Male

<div class="mt-2 text-sm opacity-70">Bird sexual differentiation reveals a mechanism fundamentally different from mammals</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500">

### The Phenomenon
- In cardinals: one side bright red (male), other side dull (female) — **split down the middle**
- Two embryos (one male, one female) fused at early stage
- In mammals, gonads would masculinize *everything* via hormones
- But birds stay split → gonads aren't running the show

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500">

### The Implication
- Art Arnold showed brain sex differences were also split by side in gynandromorphs
- In birds: the **brain itself makes hormones locally** and masculinizes itself
- Genetic sex of the brain directly determines local hormone production
- This is why Breedlove's head-transplant experiment (male brain on female body) would not have been informative

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-3 p-3 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded text-sm">

Nicole LeDouarin pioneered chick-quail chimera experiments — transplanting nervous system tissue between species. White leghorn chickens with a streak of brown quail-derived feathers confirmed the technique worked.

</div>

# Brain Plasticity: More Than We Thought

Breedlove's observation from decades of Society for Neuroscience meetings:

<div class="mt-3 p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded text-sm italic">

"Every year when I went to the neuroscience meeting, the brain was more plastic than it was the year before."

</div>

<div class="mt-4 text-sm">

**Key insights from the discussion:**

- The neocortex is probably *more* plastic than the hypothalamus — but the hypothalamus is far from hardwired
- POMC neurons in the arcuate nucleus show late-stage plasticity (hunger circuits)
- Brad Cooke showed testosterone removal can erase a sex difference in the medial amygdala in *weeks*
- Julian Davidson at Stanford showed testosterone replacement in castrated men reliably improved mood and libido — they always knew when they were getting the real thing

</div>

# The Fetal Rate of Growth

<div class="mt-2 text-sm opacity-70">Why human childhood is like being a fetus outside the womb</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

<div class="text-sm italic">

"Human beings, children are fetuses that are outside learning a whole bunch of stuff from other people. That's the real distinctiveness of our species."

</div>
<div class="text-xs opacity-60 mt-1 not-italic">— Dr. Breedlove</div>

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500">

### Human vs. Chimp Brain Growth
- Until birth: brain growth rate roughly equal in humans and chimps
- After birth: chimp brain growth **slows and asymptotes**
- Human brain continues at **fetal rate of growth** until age 6-10
- This extended window = massive social learning capacity

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500">

### What This Means for Orientation
- The brain remains hormone-sensitive for years after birth
- Behavior affects testosterone; testosterone affects the brain
- Winners of competitions get testosterone boosts; losers don't
- Even presidential election outcomes shift voters' testosterone levels

</div>

</div>

# Nature vs. Nurture: The English Analogy

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded text-lg italic">

"I speak English... I don't remember learning English and I certainly didn't choose to learn English, but I'm sure that it's English because of social influences."

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mt-2 not-italic">— Breedlove's analogy for why sexual orientation is not a choice, even if it's shaped by social forces</div>

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-3 gap-4 mt-6">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500">

### Biology Says
- Prenatal testosterone matters (digit ratios, otoacoustic emissions)
- Maternal immune response matters (fraternal birth order)
- Brain structure differs (SDNPOA/INAH-3)
- Present from birth in some markers

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500">

### Social Influence
- 95% heterosexual orientation may partly reflect a "heterosexual world" (Disney, culture)
- Culture can amplify or reduce expression of aversion
- Rough-and-tumble play: biological; most other masculine behaviors: culturally shaped

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500">

### The Bottom Line
- Researchers looked hard for social correlates of orientation — missing fathers, coddling mothers, etc.
- The data simply aren't there
- Multiple pathways; no single cause
- Not a choice — that much is clear

</div>

</div>

# Rough-and-Tumble Play: The One That Persists

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500">

### Across Species
- Males engage in much more rough-and-tumble play — rats, monkeys, humans
- Related to testosterone exposure
- Melissa Hines showed even **monkeys** prefer sex-typed toys: males go for wheeled toys, females for dolls
- Mixed groups: girls calm boys down, boys ramp girls up

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500">

### In Breedlove's Own Family
- His daughter Tessa wanted to wear dresses every day — despite her neuroscientist mother never wearing them
- One of his boys "could make a gun out of anything" — even growing up in Berkeley where toy guns were impossible to find

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 rounded text-sm italic">

"The more kids people had, the more they thought nature was important."

<div class="text-xs opacity-60 mt-1 not-italic">— Steve Glickman's observation, cited by Breedlove</div>
</div>

<div class="mt-3 text-sm opacity-70">

Rough-and-tumble play is probably the one sex difference that will persist regardless of cultural shifts — almost certainly biological in origin.

# The Trenbolone Observation

<div class="mt-2 text-sm opacity-70">An uncontrolled but striking "natural experiment" from bodybuilding culture</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500">

### The Anecdotal Pattern
- In anabolic steroid communities, **trenbolone** in particular is reported to cause:
  - Increased aggression
  - Hypersexuality
  - Self-reporting heterosexual males wanting to have sex with **males or females**
- Effect appears **reversible** when they stop
- Not controlled studies — but thousands of consistent reports

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500">

### What It Suggests
- The adult hypothalamus is still **androgen-sensitive** enough to drive powerful changes in sexual behavior and perception
- Julian Davidson's Stanford studies confirmed: testosterone replacement in castrated men reliably affects mood, energy, and libido
- Testosterone levels vary enormously between individuals — a man in his 70s can have levels comparable to someone in their 20s

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-3 p-3 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded text-sm">

This is distinct from normal testosterone replacement therapy. The observation suggests that at supraphysiological doses, specific androgens may temporarily alter the adult brain's orientation-related circuitry.

</div>

# From the Ozarks to Yale

<div class="text-sm opacity-70">Breedlove's origin story — a working-class kid from Springfield, Missouri who had never heard of Yale.</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500">

### Background
- Born in the Ozarks — "hill country," like Appalachia in terrain and culture
- Raised by grandparents: construction worker + food processing plant worker
- They'd completed about **third grade** between them
- Only income after grandfather passed: **Social Security**

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500">

### The Lucky Break
- School librarian showed him the College Board's first-ever guide
- He read the entire book — only the **financial aid** paragraphs
- Yale's entry: "we're committed to making sure everyone admitted will get the financial aid they need"

# The First Crush Exercise

Breedlove asks every class to remember their first crush — and uses it to demonstrate that orientation isn't a choice.

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500">

### Breedlove's Story
- Age 6 or 7
- Marilyn Monroe on TV — a close-up
- "Afterwards, I'm just so agitated... I had a hard time going to sleep"
- Knew nothing about sex
- Didn't choose to have that reaction

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500">

### The Pattern
- Almost always **before puberty** — so puberty isn't the cause
- The sex of the first crush tends to predict lifelong orientation
- Huberman: "It's been constant" — his first crush at age 6
- No one *decides* to feel this way
- "Sexual orientation is not a choice"

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 rounded text-sm">

The time lag is the truly strange part: something that happened before birth shapes a feeling that doesn't emerge until age 6-10. As Breedlove puts it — this was like "a visitation."

</div>
