# Why Companies Stay Private: Three Barriers

<div class="grid grid-cols-3 gap-3 mt-6 text-sm leading-relaxed">

<div class="p-3 rounded bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500">
<strong>1. Sarbanes-Oxley</strong>
<div class="text-xs mt-1 opacity-70">
"Increased fixed costs. When you introduce regulation, you increase fixed costs, and then you need to drive scale to be able to be successful. And those fixed costs are generally the same for everyone, so the big get bigger."
</div>
</div>

<div class="p-3 rounded bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500">
<strong>2. Nuisance Lawsuits</strong>
<div class="text-xs mt-1 opacity-70">
"The amount of nuisance lawsuits and claims and annoyance and just people extracting value out of public companies from the legal community has gotten surprisingly high." Texas now requires minimum share % to sue.
</div>
</div>

<div class="p-3 rounded bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500">
<strong>3. VC Incentives</strong>
<div class="text-xs mt-1 opacity-70">
"Management fees are higher in private markets — 2% on a big pool of capital. You can engineer your exit and control your exit price." VCs can unload shares at higher valuations than public comps.
</div>
</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 rounded bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 text-sm text-center">
<strong>80% of companies in this country with over $100 million of revenue are private.</strong> That doesn't seem right.

<div class="mt-4 text-sm leading-relaxed">

# Bitcoin: The Inert Asset Conundrum

<div class="mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 mb-4">

<div class="text-lg italic">
"Bitcoin is an inert asset. It is not a yielding asset. And so when you start offering to investors a yield on an underlying asset that does not yield, it's hard."
</div>
<div class="text-xs opacity-60 mt-1">— Chris Perkins, CoinFund</div>

</div>

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 mt-2 text-sm">

<div class="p-3 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500">
<strong>The Fundamental Mismatch</strong>
<div class="text-xs mt-1 opacity-70 leading-relaxed">
Strategy needs Bitcoin to go up > 11.5% per year just to cover preferred dividends. Selling BTC to pay yield reduces total Bitcoin held. Period, full stop. As Bitcoin's market cap grows, historical growth rates compress.
</div>
</div>

<div class="p-3 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500">
<strong>Other Chains Have an Edge</strong>
<div class="text-xs mt-1 opacity-70 leading-relaxed">
Ethereum, Solana, Avalanche, Hyperliquid all generate organic yield (2.7-3% staking). This makes structured products easier to design. Bitcoin's inert nature is unique — similar to trying to generate yield on gold.
</div>
</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-3 text-xs opacity-50 text-center">
"It's just a fundamental mismatch in Bitcoin that you don't have in other places." — Chris Perkins
</div>

# Why Institutions Stay Away: ADL

<div class="mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500">
<div class="text-sm font-bold">ADL = Auto-Deleveraging</div>
<div class="text-xs mt-1 opacity-70 leading-relaxed">
When the exchange says "sorry, I'm ripping up your position — there's too much risk in the system." Your hedge disappears when you need it most. In traditional markets, this is called "recovery and resolution" — but it sits behind massive risk-based capital pools. It almost never happens in TradFi.
</div>
</div>

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-3 gap-3 mt-4 text-sm text-center">

<div class="p-3 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500">
<strong>Regulation</strong>
<div class="text-xs mt-1 opacity-70">No onshore licenses → keeps most institutional capital out</div>
</div>

<div class="p-3 rounded bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500">
<strong>Security</strong>
<div class="text-xs mt-1 opacity-70">Open protocols attract bad actors. Need AI-driven security + insurance layers</div>
</div>

<div class="p-3 rounded bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500">
<strong>Risk Management</strong>
<div class="text-xs mt-1 opacity-70">Can't take counterparty risk on derivatives. Need better waterfalls than ADL</div>
</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-3 text-xs opacity-60 text-center">
"Direct-to-retail model going straight to ADL is never a thing regulators are going to be comfortable with." — Austin Campbell
</div>

# Banks Are Out of Touch: The Deposit Rate Debate

<div class="mt-4 text-sm leading-relaxed">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500 mb-4">

<div class="text-lg italic">
"Young Americans hate you. They view you as a bunch of paternalistic dickheads who crashed the system in 2008."
</div>
<div class="text-xs opacity-60 mt-1">— Austin Campbell, quoting one of his students about big banks</div>

</div>

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 mt-2 text-sm">

<div class="p-3 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500">
<strong>Why Checking Pays Zero</strong>
<div class="text-xs mt-1 opacity-70 leading-relaxed">
It's not because banks can't. It's because large depositors above FDIC limits trust "fortress balance sheets like JPMorgan" with G-SIB status. The government creates the very reason deposit rates are so low — by conferring too-big-to-fail status. Small community banks actually offer higher rates because they have to compete.
</div>
</div>

<div class="p-3 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500">
<strong>The Competitive Threat</strong>
<div class="text-xs mt-1 opacity-70 leading-relaxed">
Stablecoins backed 1:1 by T-bills or money market funds offer higher rates with full backing. "Here's your efficient frontier." Banks are fractionally reserved with an implicit guarantee — not a competitive model when the alternative is fully backed and pays yield. SoFi has opened more accounts than all big four combined.
</div>
</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-3 text-xs opacity-50 text-center">
Proposal from the show: require banks to pay risk-free rate on checking accounts if they want FDIC insurance.
