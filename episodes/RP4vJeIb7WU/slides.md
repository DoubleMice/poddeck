# Jessica's Journey: From IC PM to Chief of Staff

<div class="mt-4 space-y-4">

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded">

**The frustration**: As an IC PM at Slack, some of Jessica's ideas got funded and backed, while others she deeply believed in "died on the vine." She couldn't figure out how decisions were being made behind the scenes.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

**The bold move**: Eight and a half months pregnant, she approached incoming CPO April Underwood and said she'd love to be her chief of staff. After maternity leave, she got the role — and later served as chief of staff to Tamar Yehoshua (now CPO at Atlassian).

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded">

**The revelation**: People completely misunderstand how executives make decisions. Instead of understanding where the exec is coming from, PMs center their own desires, their own motivations, and their own slice of the world.

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 text-lg italic border-l-4 border-purple-500 bg-purple-50 bg-opacity-20 rounded">

"I was so confused and frustrated honestly because I felt like, hey, I really get this user base. I really understand what's going to move the ball forward."

</div>

# The Strobe Light Calendar

Executives are not thinking about your project.

<div class="mt-4 space-y-3">

<div class="p-3 bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500 rounded text-sm">

**You** prepped for 2-3 weeks for this review

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded text-sm">

**They** haven't thought about you since the last meeting — "they may not have gone to the bathroom today"

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded text-sm">

**The fix**: Take 30 seconds to set context. Why are we here? What happened last time? Why does this matter?

# The PM Trap: Centering Yourself Instead of the Exec

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-6">

<div class="p-4 rounded border-l-4 border-red-400 bg-red-50 bg-opacity-30">

### What PMs Do Wrong

- Go in looking for approval for their plan
- Prove they did their homework (boring the exec)
- Center their own ideas, own motivations, own slice of the world
- Treat it like a rubber stamp

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded border-l-4 border-green-500 bg-green-50 bg-opacity-30">

### What Great PMs Do

- Go in to learn and strengthen their plan
- Use the exec's domain expertise and context
- Think about how to get the best out of the exec
- Build product alongside them, not despite them

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-6 p-4 text-lg italic border-l-4 border-blue-500 bg-blue-50 bg-opacity-20 rounded">

"As product managers, one of our best sets of skills is curiosity and empathy and trying to understand our users. But the moment that we're talking to an executive, we forget those skills and those talents."

</div>

# Politics vs. Influence

This isn't about being manipulative. It's the opposite.

<div class="mt-4 space-y-3 text-sm">

<div class="p-3 bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500 rounded">

**Politics** = manipulating outcomes and people for your own gain

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

**Influence** = increasing the odds that your good ideas survive

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 text-sm">

Jessica's reframe: treat stakeholder conversations as **discovery interviews** — a way to strengthen your ideas, not sell them.

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded text-sm italic">

Noah Weiss (CPO of Slack) kept a notebook of things he learned from Stewart Butterfield — not to show off, but for "genuine learning and growth of his own ideas."

</div>

# "It's Not My Fault, but It Is My Problem"

<div class="mt-2 text-sm opacity-60">Annie Pearl (Jessica's first PM manager at Box, former CPO at Calendly and Glassdoor) taught her this frame.</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-6">

<div class="p-5 rounded border-l-4 border-red-400 bg-red-50 bg-opacity-30">

### The Ego Trap

People say: "Oh, they just don't get it. They don't see what I see. They're not in my meetings. They don't know how hard this is."

</div>

<div class="p-5 rounded border-l-4 border-green-500 bg-green-50 bg-opacity-30">

### The Growth Mindset

If you respect them, if you think they got to their position because they learned and developed real skill — take the feedback, ask questions, and don't treat it like a rubber stamp.

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-6 p-5 text-lg italic border-l-4 border-orange-500 bg-orange-50 bg-opacity-20 rounded">

"If you don't respect that person, if you don't believe they know something you don't, you need to quit. You need to go work somewhere else."

</div>

# You Get Paid to Have an Opinion

<div class="mt-2 text-sm opacity-60">Being a learner does NOT mean being a yes-man.</div>

<div class="mt-6 space-y-4">

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded">

**You are the domain expert.** Your executive is looking for you to be the deepest person in the room. Bringing your expertise to bear is absolutely crucial.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

**Have a customer story in your back pocket.** Elon Frank (CPO at Checker, ex-Slack) always had a customer anecdote ready: "I was just on site last week with so-and-so and they said..." He brought enterprise expertise that many Slack execs didn't have.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded">

**Marry expertise with curiosity.** When you hear something you disagree with, ask: "That's so interesting. What led you to believe that?" This is genuinely curious — not passive-aggressive. It co-creates with the person who offered the opinion.

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 rounded text-sm">

**Key insight**: Execs are so good at seeming certain. They say things with confidence because so much of their work is making ultra-fast decisions with little information. But if they have a growth mindset, you can help them unpack why they believe something.

</div>

# How to Present Your Ideas

Don't lead with your homework. Lead with your recommendation.

<div class="mt-4 space-y-3 text-sm">

<div class="p-3 bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500 rounded">

**Anti-pattern**: "We talked to 16 participants from 15 geos and this is statistically significant..." — the exec is bored, reaches for phone.

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

**Pattern**: Start with your recommendation. Show 2-3 options. Put evidence in appendix.

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded">

**"Stuart + 2 more"**: After a design review with Stewart Butterfield, do exactly what he asked for — plus two other versions you feel good about. Then debate the merits.

</div>

# The 30-Second Meeting Opener

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">The moment you go over 60 seconds, you've lost them.</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-4 gap-3 mt-6">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-2 border-blue-200 text-center">

### Step 1
<div class="text-sm mt-2">We're here to discuss <strong>XYZ</strong></div>

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-2 border-green-200 text-center">

### Step 2
<div class="text-sm mt-2">Last time we met, we left off <strong>here</strong></div>

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-orange-50 border-2 border-orange-200 text-center">

### Step 3
<div class="text-sm mt-2">The goals of today's meeting are <strong>ZYX</strong></div>

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-purple-50 border-2 border-purple-200 text-center">

### Step 4
<div class="text-sm mt-2">Was there anything else you were hoping to <strong>cover</strong>?</div>

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-6 p-4 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 rounded">

**Pro tip for doc-based meetings**: After the silent read, bubble up a "themes for discussion" section. Anything factual (timeline, staffing) can be answered offline. Spend the live time on the most controversial and spicy topics — that's where you get the real insight.

</div>

# Don't Ask "What's Top of Mind?" — Ask Spicier Questions

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500 rounded">

**Hot take from Jessica**: "What's top of mind?" has become a generic, uninteresting trope. It's lost its firepower.

</div>

<div class="mt-4 text-sm opacity-60">Instead, use research interview techniques on your executives — they're your users here.</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded">

### Better Questions
- "Tell me what the board is pushing you on"
- "What's the most urgent priority you're scared about messing up?"
- "What pressures are you facing?"
- "What are the key inputs to your success?"

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

### Use the People Around Them
- Their EA, chief of staff
- People who have successfully pitched ideas in the past
- Ask: "What worked? What do you think they're worried about?"
- Jessica could count on one hand how many people asked for advice before going into a product review

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded text-sm">

**AI-powered prep**: A colleague at Webflow trained a GPT on transcripts from past product reviews. PMs run their PRDs through it to ask "What's Rachel going to push back on?"

</div>

# "Hey Stuart, What Do You Think?"

<div class="mt-2 text-sm opacity-60">An early alignment technique from Slack's product culture.</div>

<div class="mt-6 space-y-4">

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded">

**The problem**: Teams were coming to Stewart Butterfield with polished, finished designs. His reaction was often: "What the [expletive] is this? This is completely different than how I had this in my mind."

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

**The fix**: Ali Rayl (early Slack employee) implemented "Hey Stuart, what do you think?" sessions. Half an hour, no finished designs — just downloading his expertise, belief system, and past experience. Start with a user interview of the exec.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded">

**Webflow's version**: "Office hours" — have strategic alignment conversations as early as possible, maybe even before you have a one-pager.

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 rounded text-sm">

**The key principle**: Go in to learn, not to convince. The earlier you do that, the better. When you engage execs this way, they co-create the solution rather than gatekeeping it.

</div>

# Align Your Incentives with Theirs

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-6">

<div class="space-y-3">

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded">

**What are they measured on?** OKRs, board pressure, revenue targets, user satisfaction — connect what you're building to the metric they're responsible for.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

**Execs want to be successful too.** They want to be good at their jobs. Show how your proposal amplifies their success so both of you win.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded">

**If you're not aligned, have that conversation.** Misalignment from the start means nothing else will work.

</div>

</div>

<div class="space-y-3">

<div class="p-4 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded">

**Don't just align on pitches — align your roadmap.** Deeply understand how OKRs got to be the way they are. Embed that incentive structure into your team's culture.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 rounded">

**Make your metrics ladder up clearly.** You may not directly move enterprise revenue, but show that "painting the inside of the cabinets" leads to retention, which leads to revenue.

</div>

</div>

</div>

# The Customer Love Sprint: A Story of Aligned Incentives

<div class="mt-4 space-y-4">

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded">

**Context**: After becoming chief of staff, Jessica took over Slack's core product team (channels, emoji, search, messaging). Leadership felt they had "lost their mojo around product craft" — they used to talk about "painting the insides of the cabinets."

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

**The initiative**: The "customer love sprint" — engineering stops everything for two weeks. Only rule: engineers pick what they work on, and they must ship something good for users.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded">

**The result**: They shipped 65 improvements in one sprint. A fun judging competition that executives participated in brought back the culture of craft they'd been missing.

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded">

**Why it worked**: It directly aligned with what executives believed was Slack's differentiation — exceptional usability. The initiative spoke their language and reinforced the company narrative, while also genuinely improving the product.

</div>

# Follow the Subtle Threads

Execs drop breadcrumbs. The best people pick them up.

<div class="mt-4 space-y-3 text-sm">

<div class="p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

**Good example**: Webflow's head of design heard their CPO mention design reviews. Within an hour, he had a Loom with a framework for high-risk vs low-risk design changes. She didn't ask him to do that.

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500 rounded">

**Bad example**: Jessica heard her CPO Tamar ask for the top 10 use cases four times. The fourth time, Tamar got really frustrated: "We've talked about this so many times before. Why don't we have this documented?"

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded">

**Speed matters**: If you wait a week for follow-up items, the exec has moved on and you've missed your chance.

</div>

# Not Every Exec Request Is a Mandate

<div class="mt-4 space-y-4">

<div class="p-4 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded">

**The classic over-reaction**: An exec says "I wonder if we could do this kind of study" and the team drops everything to run that study right now.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded">

**The right question to ask**: "That's a really cool idea. Do you think that's more important than these three other projects we're working on now?" More often than not, they'll say: "Oh, no no. That's just like a random idea. Put it on the backlog."

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

**Ask for the urgency level**: "How strongly do you feel about this?" / "How urgent do you think that is?" Execs are moving so quickly that they won't always give you context of urgency — you have to ask for it.

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded">

**Jessica is working on this herself as a leader**: "I've been working on getting better at labeling my requests — this is just an idea, this is a mandate, this is something I want to see. But it's hard sometimes because your brain is spinning with ideas."

</div>

# Building Trust: Shrink the Change

The book *Switch* calls it "shrinking the change" — make scary things feel small.

<div class="mt-4 space-y-3 text-sm">

<div class="p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded">

Make it an **experiment**, a one-week proof of concept — something that feels really small.

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

Set **milestones** where you show outcomes and fit before asking for the bigger bet.

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded">

Manage the **desire for certainty**: "I don't know the answer right now. I'm going to check in with you on X date." At least there's certainty about the next check-in.

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded">

Ask: **What type of risk** are they most afraid of? Time to market? Customer failure? Wasted resources?

</div>

</div>

# Kill Things to Build Trust

<div class="mt-4 p-4 text-lg italic border-l-4 border-blue-500 bg-blue-50 bg-opacity-20 rounded">

"One of the biggest things you can do to build trust is kill things. Deprioritize things."

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-5 mt-4">

<div class="p-3 rounded border-l-4 border-green-500 bg-green-50 bg-opacity-30">

### Why Killing Works

- Shows aligned incentives with the executive — company outcome over personal gain
- Everyone else acquires more resources — you stand out by cutting
- Red team your own ideas, especially prototypes you've fallen in love with

</div>

<div class="p-3 rounded border-l-4 border-orange-500 bg-orange-50 bg-opacity-30">

### How to Kill Well

- Come with data: "Here's how I'm going to know it's not working"
- Set a date: "We'll come back to you on X date with a decision"
- Think like a CPO: you have to act like a CPO, come in with that perspective, and do it quickly

</div>

</div>

# Constraints Aren't Fixed — Execs Can Move Levers

<div class="mt-6 space-y-4">

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded">

**What people miss**: Execs don't have the same boundaries of budget, headcount, and timeline that you feel. They can move people around, ask for more resourcing, elevate or kill projects.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

**The 10x pitch**: "With today's resourcing, here's what I can get. But I'm thinking of the accelerated case — if that's what you want and aligned with your incentives, here's what I need to be successful."

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded">

**Ask for what you need**: "I need eight more people. I need you twice a week for an hour. I need much closer alignment with our marketing team." People miss out on this all the time — especially when the ask from the executive seems unreasonable.

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded">

**The unlock**: If you get them excited enough, everything changes in terms of resourcing and prioritization. You're not just asking for what you need — you're helping them see the opportunity so big they want to go big on it too.

</div>

# Broaden Your Context — Think Like a CPO

<div class="mt-4 space-y-4">

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded">

**How leaders got to their role**: They broadened their context. They had great "peripheral vision." To show up more senior, keep your domain expertise but broaden your perspective to think about the whole ecosystem, organization, and industry.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

**Product citizenship**: Webflow's PM career ladder explicitly notes that becoming more senior means embedding back in the organization — sharing best practices, mentoring others, broadening horizons on tooling, technology, and strategy.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded">

**The litmus test**: Jessica sees docs that say "our KR is to ship this thing" — and asks, "Why does that matter? Why would the CEO be excited about shipping that?" If you can't connect it to a business or user outcome, you might need to change course.

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 rounded">

**The ultimate trust move**: Tell leaders "I don't think this is the highest leverage thing right now. We could actually do X to be much more effective for our broader goals." That's thinking like a CPO.

</div>

# Building Cross-Functional Buy-In

<div class="mt-4 text-sm opacity-60">Influence isn't just about the product exec — it's about the entire organization.</div>

<div class="mt-6 space-y-4">

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded">

**Execs also want to hear from their peers.** If customer success thinks your idea will accelerate expansion rates, or marketing thinks signups would be 10x'd — that cross-functional alignment creates a groundswell of buy-in.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

**Build the V1 yourself.** With today's tools, prototype something, build the messy version, get feedback, iterate. Be socializing it in a way that creates momentum — to the point where the exec can't say no to the funding.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded">

**The whiteboard marker trick** (from April Underwood): In strategy meetings with Stewart, April always held the whiteboard marker. She was extracting his instincts while packaging them into translatable, actionable frameworks — marrying what she knew about the world with what he knew.

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

The act of influence, stakeholder management, and learning is the **10x skill**.

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded">

What can you notice that AI can't? "AI is not good at being an anthropologist yet."

# Strategy Clarity > PRDs

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500 rounded">

**Jessica's hot take on Twitter discourse**: Takes like "PRDs are dead" or "software is dead" are "really dumb" and "a little insufferable." What still matters: understanding users, understanding business dynamics, understanding strategy, and thinking through the right way to execute together.

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 mt-6">

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded">

### Why Strategy Matters More Now

- Ideas proliferate, building happens faster
- But **mistakes compound faster** too
- A corpus of shared beliefs — updated frequently — is what enables teams to build the right stuff

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

### The Distribution Problem

- We're going to have so much software we won't know what to do with it
- Distribution becomes the bottleneck: who has marketing dollars, brand reputation, existing customer base
- Trust — with users and internal teams — is a function of influence and empathy

</div>

</div>

**Lenny's observation**: Once you have strategy clarity, "cool, fire off 100 agents and we'll go build it and launch it." The deciding-what and the getting-aligned are the human bottleneck now.

# Be Authentic to Yourself

<div class="mt-4 space-y-4">

<div class="p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded">

**All personality types can influence.** You could be shy and introverted, or super data-oriented and technical. Every type can build trust and influence in the way that feels true to them.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

**Jessica's experience**: Early in her career, she got feedback like "How do you show up more like a man would? More aggressively, more decisively." There were some good nuggets, but it took her a while to realize that who she is and how she shows up is her superpower.

</div>

<div class="p-4 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded">

**Her family motto — "First the guests"**: Be of service to others, be welcoming, be kind. Put them first. "When you are of service to others, when you are inclusive, when you can put them first ahead of yourself, I think it actually brings a lot of joy to you."

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded text-sm italic">

"What I need to do is think about what I want to accomplish and how I want to grow and do that in a way that is true to me."
