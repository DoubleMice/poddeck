# Expanding the Vocabulary of Emotion

<div class="mt-2 text-sm opacity-60">Paul Ekman's original 6 emotions were a breakthrough — but 34 years of research reveals far more.</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded border-l-4 border-blue-400 bg-blue-50 bg-opacity-30">

### The Old Canon (Ekman, 1970s)
6 basic emotions studied via static photographs:
- Anger, Fear, Sadness
- Disgust, Surprise, Happiness

One hour to code one minute of facial muscle movements — painstakingly slow science.

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded border-l-4 border-green-500 bg-green-50 bg-opacity-30">

### The New Taxonomy (Keltner + Cowan)
AI-coded facial expressions across cultures reveal **20 distinct states**:
- Laughter, love, compassion, **awe**
- Embarrassment, shame, pain
- And many more previously unnamed states

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 rounded text-sm">

**Key finding**: Grad student Alan Cowan used AI with Google engineers to code **2 million videos from 144 cultures** — found **75% overlap** across cultures in how we express emotions like awe at fireworks or concentration on a test.

</div>

# The Emotion Taxonomy

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-3">From 6 to 20: a revolution in how we map human feeling.</div>

- Ekman's 6 emotions dominated the field for decades
- Keltner's estimate: **50 to 60%** of facial expressions are hardwired through evolution
- The rest varies across cultures in "interesting ways"
- New tools: gaze patterns, face coloration, breath patterns, vagal tone, gut activation, voice

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded italic text-sm">

Keltner chose to study emotions based on "what's happening out in our lives" — not what's convenient in a lab.

# What Is Awe? The "Small to Vast" Shift

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">Awe isn't elusive — it has a precise perceptual signature.</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-2">

<div>

### The Core Mechanism

Keltner learned from Pixar's Pete Doctor: in animated films, the camera starts **narrow** — a certain kind of attention, sympathetic, fearful — and then suddenly reveals **the vastness** of something.

Huberman connects this to vision science: when our visual aperture widens (seeing a horizon), we become more parasympathetic. We relax.

</div>

<div>

### The Evidence

<div class="p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded mb-3">

**Yosemite study**: Hundreds of travelers asked "How do you feel about yourself right now?" answered: "I feel small and quiet, but part of something really large."

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded mb-3">

**T-Rex skeleton study**: People standing near a replica on the Berkeley campus became "expansive and collective."

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded">

**Giant eucalyptus trees**: Another natural laboratory for studying awe in the field.

</div>

</div>

</div>

# Small → Expand → Vast

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-3">The fundamental perceptual shift behind awe.</div>

- **Small**: Narrow visual aperture, high alertness, fine-slicing time
- **Vast**: Wide aperture, parasympathetic activation, expanded time perception
- The shift itself is what triggers awe — not being in one state

<div class="mt-3 p-3 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded text-sm">

Huberman's insight: "It's not about having a small aperture or a big aperture per se — it's the process of going from one aperture to the next."

</div>

<div class="mt-2 p-3 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded text-sm">

A conductor told Keltner: "What's the secret? Why awe and music?" His answer: **"Time. It's all about what it does to our sense of time."**

# The Awe Walk: A Simple Weekly Practice

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-3">A protocol anyone can do — with measurable health outcomes over weeks and years.</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-2">

<div>

### The Protocol

1. **Go on your regular walk** — once a week, half an hour
2. **Go somewhere you wouldn't ordinarily go** — a place that may surprise you
3. **Slow down** and deepen your breathing, sync it with your walking
4. **Go from small to vast** — look at clouds, then the whole pattern of clouds; look at a tree, then the light on the trees, then patterns of light
5. Listen to one laugh, then the whole symphony of laughter of kids

</div>

<div>

### The Results

<div class="p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded mb-3">

**8-week study** (75+ year olds): More kindness, more awe, **less physical pain**

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded mb-3">

**6 years later**: Better brain health (Virginia Sturm, UCSF study)

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 rounded mb-3">

**Long COVID**: Just a minute of awe a day **reduced long COVID symptoms**

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500 rounded">

**Broader effects**: Reduced inflammation, elevated vagal tone — doctors are starting to think "I'm going to prescribe nature"

</div>

</div>

# Music: The Fastest Path to Collective Consciousness

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-3">Music synchronizes brains within milliseconds — something that is "very hard to get people to do" any other way.</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-2">

<div>

### Brain Synchronization

- A nine-month-old listening to music is **syncing up** to the sounds and rhythms of their culture's music
- At concerts and sporting events, heart rates and brains synchronize
- This is "the materialistic account of collective consciousness — we're all sharing brain patterns and awareness"

<div class="mt-3 p-3 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 rounded text-sm italic">

Susan Langer, a philosopher, called music "this tonal language of emotion and identity."

</div>

</div>

<div>

### Huberman's Punk Rock Epiphany

The show that changed everything: Rancid playing with Sick of It All, somewhere between 1993 and 1994, a little club in Santa Barbara.

<div class="p-3 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded mt-3 text-sm">

"I feel very much a part of this. I love the music. I know every lyric — and I'm a little bit frightened and I love it."

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded mt-3 text-sm">

**Collective effervescence** (Durkheim): Physical dancing, chanting, sporting events together — "syncing up with each other, feeling like we're part of this vast group."

# What Killed Collective Music?

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-3">A cautionary tale from the Grateful Dead and Laurel Canyon — the "me drug" destroyed what music built.</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div>

### The Grateful Dead Story

On Tim Armstrong's recommendation, Huberman started listening to the Grateful Dead. Armstrong's reasoning: "They're punk rockers... they played a different show every night." Every show was unique — that was the collective magic.

Then someone asked: **"What killed the collective of music?"**

<div class="p-4 bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500 rounded mt-3">

The answer: **Cocaine.**

"Cuz cocaine's all about me. It's the me drug."

</div>

</div>

<div>

### The Laurel Canyon Parallel

Keltner grew up in Laurel Canyon (1968-70) during peak creativity — Joni Mitchell, the Birds, Beach Boys, Graham Nash. After his brother passed away, he read about what changed the scene:

<div class="p-3 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded mb-3 text-sm">

The historians said the same thing — cocaine shifted everything from communal music-making to self-focused competition. The whole spirit changed.

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded text-sm">

**Huberman's neuroscience gloss**: Cocaine means dopamine and adrenaline. When those are elevated, every idea that's mine becomes the only thing that matters. Very different than cannabis, psychedelics, or the sober experience of just being with the music.

# Joe Strummer: A Case Study in Moral Beauty

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-3">One of the 8 wonders of awe is being amazed by people's courage, strength, and kindness.</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-2">

<div>

### Why Strummer Matters to Huberman

1. **Lyrics that transcend language** — like Bob Dylan, you're not sure what the song's about but it makes sense beyond the literal words
2. **Breath and intonation** — "his intonation is like unparalleled"
3. **Total authenticity** — Rick Rubin explained: "Everything he said, he brought his whole life experience into those statements"

<div class="p-3 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 rounded mt-3 text-sm">

</div>

<div>

### Strummer's Campfires

After the Clash ended, Strummer became famous for holding **campfires** — in Manhattan, by the river, with whoever would come.

- Kids, adults, famous friends and strangers
- Staying out till 2 or 3 AM, playing music, singing, drumming
- Not for recording or distribution — just for the experience

<div class="p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded mt-3 text-sm">

Keltner adds: "There's this new science of campfires — they're several hundred thousand years old." In small-scale societies, people gather around campfires to tell stories of how they're all connected.

# Embarrassment: A Signal of Moral Commitment

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-3">Why blushing is a feature, not a bug — it shows you care about the group.</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-2">

<div>

### The Discovery

Keltner was studying the startle response in Ekman's lab. After being startled, people got **embarrassed** — blush, avert gaze, hide face. Ekman told him: "That's a motor pattern of emotion. You should go study it."

Darwin had written about it: a person's embarrassment is a sign of their commitment to the collective.

<div class="p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded mt-3 text-sm">

**Research finding**: When you see people get embarrassed, you **like them more**, trust them more, give resources to them, and think they're a good group member.

</div>

</div>

<div>

### The Brain Connection

<div class="p-3 bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500 rounded mb-3">

**Orbital frontal patients**: People with damage to the orbital frontal cortex (from falls, motorcycle accidents) don't show embarrassment where they should — and others perceive them as "creepy" or not playing by the rules.

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded">

**Darwin's insight**: The blush is "a sign of your healthy character, your moral virtue almost." In nonhuman species, facial reddening is associated with physical robustness; in humans, it signals moral robustness.

</div>

</div>

</div>

# The Science of Teasing

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-3">Good teasing is group maintenance. Bad teasing is bullying. Science can pinpoint the line.</div>

**The Fraternity Study**: Groups of four fraternity guys given initials to make up nicknames — then teased each other.

Results:
- The more they got embarrassed, the **better they liked each other**
- Better teasers — more playful, funny, aware of group norms but not humiliating — were **more popular**
- The teasing surfaced group norms: "What do we as a collective really care about?"

**Jocko Willink's Rule**: Guys tease each other relentlessly **face to face** — but never behind someone's back. And they'll defend the same person against outside criticism.

# Psychedelics and Awe

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-3">Two different paths, one conclusion: psychedelics can open the door to awe — but context matters enormously.</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-2">

<div>

### Keltner's Path

Tried psychedelics in his late teens (17-19). "I was a very anxious obsessive kid and I think they opened up my mind in this perspective way we've been talking about."

He was reading Castaneda, "the doors of perception" — psychedelics embedded in a culture of trying to find mysticism.

<div class="p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded mt-3 text-sm">

**Research**: Molly Crockett at Princeton found that going to a festival with psychedelics makes you **kinder a year later** — through awe.

</div>

</div>

<div>

### Huberman's Path

The opposite experience — terrifying early encounters, stopped, then later had immensely beneficial experiences **in a therapeutic setting**.

<div class="p-3 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 rounded mb-3 text-sm">

**Both agree on microdosing**: Robin Carhart-Harris's data show no evidence of benefit from microdosing on major depression — vs. two rounds of psilocybin with guide therapy.

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500 rounded text-sm">

**The concern**: Tens of millions now using psychedelics — not in the careful "cultural container of inquiry and knowledge and guidance" that Michael Pollan describes.

</div>

</div>

</div>

# Awe Design: Engineering Wonder into Cities

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-3">Keltner is working with Gehl Architecture in Copenhagen on a "cities of awe" initiative.</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-2">

<div>

### The Opportunity

70% of the human population lives in cities. Most carbon emissions come from cities. These are places we can **redesign**.

The awe science gives a road map:
- A little **nature** — re-green parts of the city
- Some **public art** — visual beauty accessible to all
- Opportunity to recognize **moral beauty** — face-to-face interaction
- **Collective activity** — yoga in the town square, walking together
- Space for **meditation and reflection**

</div>

<div>

### The Iron Works Model

<div class="p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded mb-3 text-sm">

Keltner's climbing gym example: people climb, but there's also an **art exhibit each month** of a local artist, and music playing. Multiple sources of awe layered naturally into one space.

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded mb-3 text-sm">

**The question for any space**: Can it provide nature, art, moral beauty, collective activity, music, and reflection — the way temples and churches once did?

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded text-sm">

**Japanese onsen**: Built entire community experiences around hot springs — wash, bathe, eat together, spend time with kids. Wisdom built into the architecture.

</div>

</div>

</div>

# The Wellness Community as Collective Awe

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-3">Huberman argues that the health/wellness movement is quietly rebuilding communal life.</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-3 gap-4 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500">

### The Shift
It used to be meditation, breath work, Esalen — isolated pockets. Now people get together to sauna, to do breath work, to cold plunge. For thousands of years, humans did this. It's not new.

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500">

### The Connection
People linked on social media who also do things in the real world — morning sunlight, gym, sauna. A community being built around shared practices, visible to each other.

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500">

### CrossFit Example
Huberman remembers his ex-girlfriend's CrossFit gym: barbecues, cleaning the gym together, dressing up in costumes. "They had this awesome social community."

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 rounded text-sm">

**The deeper layer**: People who want control over their state — "because otherwise the world will take control of it for you." It's not about supplements or cold plunges per se — it's about shared practice and agency.

</div>

# Emotion, Motor Patterns, and Language: A Triad

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-3">Huberman's question: how do emotion, movement, and words relate — and can we have one without the others?</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-3 gap-4 mt-4 text-center">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-2 border-blue-200">

### Motor Patterns
<div class="text-sm mt-2">75% of emotional expression is motor — facial musculature, body, skeletal muscles</div>
<div class="text-xs opacity-60 mt-1">Darwin got this right</div>

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-2 border-green-200">

### Language
<div class="text-sm mt-2">"Oh, I'm feeling angry or ashamed or embarrassed or love" — words we put to experience</div>
<div class="text-xs opacity-60 mt-1">Correlates with motor at only ~0.2</div>

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-purple-50 border-2 border-purple-200">

### The Feeling
<div class="text-sm mt-2">The conscious experience itself — "still one of the uncharted territories of our field"</div>
<div class="text-xs opacity-60 mt-1">William James: "words are tattered fragments"</div>

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded">

**The fighting paradox**: Sam Sheridan wrote in *A Fighter's Heart* that sparring is one of the most bonding experiences — "a primitive non-language state." Both vulnerable, obeying rules, transcending language. Like a mosh pit: there's something transcendent about crashing into each other.

</div>

# The Aquarium Lesson: What We Wouldn't Do to Fish

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-3">Huberman draws a striking parallel between how he cares for his fish — and how we neglect basic human needs.</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div>

### What Huberman Does for His Fish

- Never **isolate** them from one another
- Give them enough places to **hide** (dominance hierarchy needs space)
- Keep them on a proper **light cycle**
- Feed them — but not **overfed or underfed**
- When the ecosystem is doing well: "I feel — it's a form of — it's brought me some awe at times"

</div>

<div>

### What We Do to Ourselves

<div class="p-3 bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-500 rounded mb-3 text-sm">

"I wouldn't isolate them, give them like little videos of other fish to look at. I know that wouldn't work. I know that they would die."

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded mb-3 text-sm">

"I wouldn't do most of the things that we do to ourselves to my fish."

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded text-sm">

**The insight**: We evolved similar nervous systems and similar needs. The basic units of care and community are the same across species — we just play them out differently.

</div>

</div>

</div>

# Campfires: The Ancient Technology of Connection

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-3">Several hundred thousand years old — and possibly the next frontier for rebuilding community.</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div>

### The Science

Keltner confirms: "There's this new science of campfires and they're several hundred thousand years old."

In small-scale societies, people gather at night around campfires and tell stories — stories of how they're all connected, watching out for what is dangerous in the dark. "A lot of stuff happens that's fundamental to our humanity around campfires."

<div class="p-3 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 rounded mt-3 text-sm">

**Red light therapy bonus**: Long wavelength light from fire goes through your body, is absorbed by water in mitochondria, and improves mitochondrial function — the same principle as expensive red light panels.

</div>

</div>

<div>

### Huberman's Dream

Inspired by Strummer's campfires, Huberman floats the idea of touring — a bus, town to town, science and health discussions, but mostly just **listening to people** and doing campfires.

<div class="p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded mt-3 text-sm">

Keltner's version: "My dream was to take that bus and to go to all the basketball courts of the country."

</div>

<div class="p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 rounded mt-3 text-sm">

**The sauna connection**: "Everybody sort of off-handedly notes like I love the conversations that happen in the sauna." Community emerges naturally when you share physical experience.

</div>

</div>

# Do You Believe in Life After Death?

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-3">Huberman asked only one guest this question — and it's Keltner.</div>

"I do. I do."

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div>

When Keltner's brother Ralph passed away from colon cancer, he had a profound transcendent experience that night. "I saw space in a different way. I saw something alive in him."

Afterward: the grief experience of his brother being around — "his voice, his hand was on my back."

<div class="p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 rounded mt-3 text-sm">

"I've moved from being a skeptical but open agnostic to like, yeah, there's something there that's beyond what we know."
